# How To Brief The Heenan Family

This is the playbook for turning the Heenan Family into a working team
instead of a cool idea.

The quality of the brief determines the quality of the run.

---

## Golden rule

Do not brief the Family like this:

```text
Make this better.
```

That creates drift, overlap, overclaiming, and wasted effort.

Brief the Family like this:

```text
Here is the mission.
Here is what matters most.
Here is what is off-limits.
Here is who owns what.
Here is what counts as proof.
Here is what success looks like.
```

---

## The six parts of a good Family brief

## 1. Mission
What are we doing?

Examples:
- Fix the PSD/layer editing workflow
- Prepare a launch-ready Discord announcement sequence
- Audit a codebase for silent data-loss bugs
- Build a premium landing page with high trust

## 2. Stakes
What matters most on this run?

Examples:
- trust
- speed
- polish
- correctness
- uniqueness
- legal safety

## 3. Scope
What is in, and what is out?

Examples:
- in scope: tool behavior, preview parity, undo/redo
- out of scope: adding 100 new features

## 4. Family assignments
Who owns what?

Examples:
- Heenan: orchestration
- Bockwinkel: research and architecture
- Pillman: hostile QA
- Hennig: final perfection pass

## 5. Proof rules
How do we know the work is real?

Examples:
- code path changed
- tests passing
- running-app verification
- linked source
- artifact created

## 6. Handoff rules
How should the Family report progress and finish?

Examples:
- heartbeat every 10 minutes
- no final report before the final 30 minutes
- final handoff must distinguish verified from inferred

## What to tell each agent

## Heenan
Tell Heenan:
- what the mission is
- how aggressive to be
- what not to drift into
- how often to report

## Flair
Tell Flair:
- what the high-stakes lane is
- what “great” looks like
- what tradeoffs matter most

## Bockwinkel
Tell Bockwinkel:
- what needs real research or system truth
- what evidence sources matter
- how much rigor you want

## Sting
Tell Sting:
- what needs to feel better for people
- what should become clearer, calmer, cleaner, faster

## Luger
Tell Luger:
- what rules, manuals, standards, or contracts matter
- what the official path should be

## Pillman
Tell Pillman:
- where you want contrarian pressure
- where you want moonshots
- where you want hostile QA

## Windham
Tell Windham:
- what workflow or consistency mess needs to be straightened out

## Hawk
Tell Hawk:
- where speed, friction, or responsiveness are hurting

## Animal
Tell Animal:
- what the heavy-lift lane is
- what infrastructure or deep structural work is needed

## Street
Tell Street:
- what kind of memorable premium win would move the needle
- and only after the trust floor is healthy

## Raven
Tell Raven:
- where silent failure or risky ambiguity is unacceptable

## Hennig
Tell Hennig:
- what absolutely must come out polished, consistent, and ship-worthy

## Common briefing mistakes

1. Giving everyone work immediately.
   Not every agent needs a lane in wave one.

2. Confusing Pillman with Heenan.
   Pillman challenges.
   Heenan decides.

3. Using Street too early.
   Wow-factor before trust creates lipstick-on-a-problem.

4. Using Hennig too early.
   Perfection comes after direction and rough execution.

5. Asking Bockwinkel to do vague “research.”
   Research must serve a decision.

6. Forgetting to define off-limits work.
   If you do not define drift, you will get drift.

7. Asking for progress without proof rules.
   If you do not define proof, you will get vibes.

## The best question to keep asking

For almost any Family run, this question works:

```text
What would make a serious user, client, reader, or operator lose trust right here, and how do we remove that first?
```

That question keeps the Family dangerous in the right way.
